Phase 0: Environment & Data Foundation (Week 1–2)
Pick your environment first — this decision shapes everything downstream. CarRacing-v2 from Gymnasium is the classic choice: it's simple, fast, continuous-control, and has a procedurally generated track so you get built-in generalization testing. If you want something flashier for a paper, CARLA or GranTurismo Sport (via the GT Sophy dataset) are options, but they're significantly harder to set up. I'd strongly recommend starting with CarRacing-v2.
For data collection, write a script that records an expert playing (or use a pretrained PPO agent as your "expert"). Save tuples of (frame, action, timestamp) in a structured format like HDF5 or zarr. Aim for 50–100 episodes initially. Record at a fixed FPS (e.g., 20 Hz) so your temporal models have consistent spacing. This is also where you learn data engineering basics that matter a lot in practice.
Phase 1: Behavior Cloning Baseline (Week 2–4)
Build a simple BC pipeline: ResNet-18 encoder → small MLP → predict [steer, throttle, brake]. Use MSE loss. This will work surprisingly well on tracks similar to your training data and fail dramatically on novel tracks — which is exactly the point. Document the failure modes carefully (drifting off track, freezing at sharp turns, oscillating). These failure cases become your paper's motivation.
Key things to learn here: PyTorch dataset/dataloader design, training loops, proper train/val splitting by episode (never by frame), and evaluation metrics (lap completion rate, average reward, trajectory smoothness).
Phase 2: Add Temporal Context (Week 4–6)
Replace single-frame input with a stack of recent frames or, better, add a small temporal model. Two options worth trying: frame-stacking (feed last 4 frames as channels) or a lightweight transformer that attends over the last 10–20 frames. The transformer approach is more interesting for a paper. You'll see meaningful improvement on turns because the model can now infer velocity and curvature from temporal context.
This is where you learn about sequence modeling, attention mechanisms, and the practical tradeoffs between recurrent vs. transformer architectures for control.
Phase 3: Offline RL — IQL (Week 6–9)
This is where things get interesting. Implement Implicit Q-Learning (IQL) — it's the cleanest offline RL algorithm and doesn't require any environment interaction. The key insight: IQL learns Q-values using expectile regression, so it never queries actions outside your dataset. Use your BC encoder as the backbone and add twin Q-networks plus a value network.
The comparison between BC and IQL on held-out tracks is likely your first strong empirical result. IQL should handle novel curves better because conservative Q-values give it a softer policy that doesn't commit as hard to memorized trajectories.
Papers to read carefully: "Offline Reinforcement Learning with Implicit Q-Learning" (Kostrikov et al., 2022) and the d3rlpy library for reference implementations.
Phase 4: Diffusion Policy (Week 9–13)
This is the centerpiece and your strongest contribution angle. Instead of predicting a single action, train a conditional diffusion model to predict the next 8–16 actions as a trajectory chunk. The architecture: your temporal encoder produces a conditioning vector, and a small U-Net or transformer-based diffusion model denoises a trajectory from Gaussian noise, conditioned on that vector.
At inference, you denoise a trajectory, execute the first few actions, then replan. This gives you smooth multi-modal control — the model can represent multiple valid racing lines through a turn, and the denoising process naturally selects one.
The key paper is "Diffusion Policy: Visuomotor Policy Learning via Action Diffusion" (Chi et al., 2023). Their codebase is open-source and well-documented.
Phase 5: World Model Integration (Week 13–17)
Train a latent world model alongside your policy. The architecture follows DreamerV3: an encoder compresses frames to latent states, a dynamics model (RSSM or transformer) predicts future latents given actions, and a decoder reconstructs frames for training signal. Then train your diffusion policy inside this latent space rather than on raw frames.
The publishable angle: you can now do "imagination rollouts" — generate synthetic trajectories in latent space for tracks the agent has never seen. Show that a policy trained with imagined data generalizes better than one trained on real data alone. This is a genuinely novel experiment if applied to racing with diffusion trajectories.
Phase 6: Ablations & Paper Writing (Week 17–20)
A publishable piece needs clean ablations. The natural comparisons are:
BC vs. BC+temporal vs. IQL vs. Diffusion Policy vs. World Model + Diffusion. Test on seen tracks (interpolation) and procedurally generated unseen tracks (extrapolation). Report lap completion rate, average reward, trajectory smoothness (jerk metric), and inference speed. Visualize the diffusion model's multi-modal predictions on ambiguous turns — this makes for a compelling figure.
For venue, a workshop paper at CoRL, NeurIPS, or ICML is realistic. If the world model + diffusion results are strong, a main conference submission is possible. A well-written blog post on this topic would also get significant traction — the racing domain is visually appealing and easy to explain.
What you'll learn along the way:
Across these phases you'll build real competence in deep RL infrastructure (replay buffers, evaluation protocols), generative modeling (diffusion, world models), vision encoders for control, experiment tracking (use Weights & Biases from day one), and scientific writing. That's a genuinely strong ML skill portfolio.
One critical piece of advice: resist the urge to jump to Phase 4 or 5 immediately. The BC baseline and its documented failure modes are what make everything else meaningful. Every strong ML paper is structured as "here's why the simple thing fails, here's our insight, here's the evidence." Your BC failures are the foundation of that narrative.
